About

Morgan David Yadron recently graduated magna cum laude from the University of Arizona with a B.A. in Psychology and a minor in Philosophy. With a GPA of 3.7, his academic work reflects a deep commitment to exploring the intersections of mind, morality, behavior, and meaning. His primary interests include religious philosophy, ancient philosophy, deontology, and metaphysics—especially where psychological theory and philosophical inquiry converge.

During his undergraduate years, Morgan was actively involved in campus life, contributing to both academic and extracurricular communities centered on dialogue, reflection, and service. He is currently pursuing graduate studies in philosophy at the Dominican School of Philosophy and Theology in Berkeley, California, where he aims to develop a robust understanding of the human condition through both analytic and historical lenses. Currently, Morgan is studying and engaging in the critical concepts of moral realism, Thomistic Natural Law, objective morality, and morality's relation to human evolution, anthropology, and the cultivation of character.

In addition to his formal philosophical training, Morgan has undertaken international travel aimed at deepening his understanding of Western intellectual and religious traditions. His time in Italy—visiting cities such as Rome, Florence, Venice, Milan, and Naples—and Switzerland’s St. Moritz has provided firsthand engagement with the historical and cultural landscape shaped by Catholicism and Christianity. While in Rome, Morgan met with clergy and explored ecclesiastical sites, gaining unique historical and theological insight into the origins and propagation of the Christian faith. These experiences have meaningfully informed his academic work, particularly in the areas of religious philosophy, metaphysics, and moral theology.

Thank you for stopping by, and feel free to browse through the CV, essays, talks, and updates on my work.

    Featured Talks and Videos

    The Problem of Evil: A Psychological and Philosophical Inquiry: April, 2025

    A cross-disciplinary exploration of classical theodicy and psychological theory, this talk investigates the tension between suffering, moral agency, and belief in a benevolent deity. Using psychological and philosophical perspectives, and drawing from prominent names such as Nietzsche and Dostoevsky, this talk aims to reconcile the existence of natural evil and moral evil with that of a benevolent god.

    God or Prophet?: A Critical Analysis into the Divinity of Jesus Christ

    Was Jesus merely a prophetic apocalyptic Jew, or was he truly the Son of God? This video offers a critical and nuanced exploration of one of history’s most consequential and important figures — Jesus of Nazareth — through the lenses of history, theology, and philosophy. Drawing on the works of scholars such as N.T. Wright, Larry Hurtado, C.S. Lewis, Albert Schweitzer, David Alan Black, Bart Ehrman, and Richard Bauckham, I examine the historical Jesus, the early Church’s explosive rise through the lens of Paul, and the enduring question of Jesus’ divinity. This presentation incorporates both canonical texts (New American Bible Revised Edition) and relevant non-canonical sources to weigh the evidence with honesty and intellectual fairness.

    The Historicity of Christianity and Christian Apologetics: Talk Transcript, February, 2025

    A live apologetics talk delivered in a pastoral fashion at the University of Arizona’s Newman Center, this presentation examines the historical, fundemental, and psychological foundations of Christianity. Using philosophical reasoning, early textual evidence, and cognitive science, it argues for the plausibility and truth of the Gospel accounts, culminating in a robust defense of Christ’s resurrection and its implications for human freedom and hope.

    Academic Foundations

    During my time at the University of Arizona, I was involved in a range of academic opportunities that helped shape my philosophical and psychological interests. These experiences formed the foundation for my current graduate studies and future research aspirations.

    • Preceptorship in Religion and Spirituality Class, RELIGION 336 – Assisted in undergraduate instruction, facilitated discussions, and supported students in coursework. In addition to being a facilitator for conversations regarding class material, I acted as a mentor and guide for any questions regarding psychology, spirituality, religion, phenomenology, moral questions, metaphysical-related material, and more.
    • Research Assistant in Consciousness Action-Time Lab – Discussed various journals and published papers relating to human consciousness, split-brain syndrome and split consciousness, timed-reactions in labs, and depression/anxiety symptoms and their relationship to timed-reactions and consciousness. Presented information on split-brain syndrome, as well as its relation/application in lab settings. Facilitated conversations regarding the application of lab settings and their implications, and devised lab settings in relation to timed reactions and consciousness.
    • Research Assistant in Human Memory Lab – Transcribed audio files respective to different participants, pertaining to stories induced by cues, images, and particular words. These audio files were then used for data analysis.
    • Academic Presentations – Delivered formal and informal lectures at The Catholic Newman Center in Tucson, Arizona on topics including the problem of evil, early Christian scripture, and the intersection of faith and science.
